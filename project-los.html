Patient Length of Stay Prediction

Optimizing Resource Allocation in Hospitals
Jun – Aug 2025 Python, SQL, XGBoost, Power BI Role: Data Science Intern

The Challenge: Unpredictability

Hospitals worldwide encounter significant challenges in managing bed capacity due to the unpredictability of patient Length of Stay (LOS). Variations in patient volume lead to overcrowding and inefficient resource utilization.

Goal: "To build a machine learning model that accurately predicts LOS based on initial diagnosis, severity of illness, and treatment plans, enabling proactive resource allocation."

Data Engineering & Analysis

We analyzed a comprehensive dataset covering patient demographics, admission types, and clinical severity measures. Multicollinearity was a significant challenge.

Variance Inflation Factor (VIF)

To ensure model stability, we performed a Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) analysis to detect and remove highly correlated variables. This step was crucial for isolating the true drivers of LOS.

Selected Features

  • Clinical: Severity of Illness, Severity Scores, CCSR Diagnosis Code.
  • Operational: Type of Admission (Emergency vs. Elective), Treatment Type.
  • Demographics: Patient Age, Gender.

Modeling Strategy: XGBoost

We treated this as a regression problem. Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) was selected as the optimal model due to its ability to handle non-linear relationships and its robustness against outliers.

Performance Metrics

  • Accuracy: Achieved a prediction accuracy of 93.3%.
  • AUC Score: 0.84, indicating strong discriminatory power between short and long stays.
  • Hyperparameter Tuning: Optimized learning rate and tree depth to prevent overfitting.

Visualization & Strategic Impact

To make the model insights accessible to hospital administrators, we built an interactive Power BI Dashboard. This tool allows stakeholders to visualize bed occupancy trends and anticipate discharge bottlenecks.

Business Value

  • Resource Allocation: Hospitals can allocate nursing staff and beds proactively based on predicted discharges.
  • Patient Categorization: Patients are categorized into "Low LOS" (likely to discharge soon) vs "High LOS" (complex care), enabling tailored care pathways.
