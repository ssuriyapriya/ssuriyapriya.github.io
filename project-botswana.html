Botswana Obesity Study

Analyzing Telemetry Data from "Fooya!" Mobile Game
Research Project Python, Markov Chains, Telemetry Role: Researcher & Data Scientist

The Challenge: Pediatric Obesity

Pediatric obesity is a growing global crisis driven by unhealthy dietary habits and sedentary lifestyles. Traditional educational interventions often fail to engage children. This study explored implicit learning via a mobile game called Fooya! to influence children's food choices without direct "lecturing."

Study Goal: "To investigate key gameplay interactions that lead to food-related decision improvements by analyzing telemetry data from a Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT)."

We conducted the study with 90 students in Chennai, India, randomized into a treatment group (playing Fooya!) and a control group (playing Uno).

Predicting Dietary Choices (ML)

We built predictive models to classify whether a child would make a Healthy Choice (1) or Unhealthy Choice (0) based on their game performance and demographics.

Feature Engineering

  • Demographics: BMI Category (WHO classification), Age Groups (binned 9-11).
  • Telemetry Features: Game Level reached, Usage of Shield, Calories Status.
  • Preprocessing: Applied One-Hot Encoding for categorical variables and StandardScaler for normalization.

Modeling Strategy

We implemented a Voting Classifier combining Logistic Regression (for interpretability) and Random Forest (for non-linear interactions). The ensemble approach improved robustness against class imbalances.

Telemetry & Markov Analysis

To understand player behavior, we modeled game progression using Markov Chains and absorbing-state analysis. This revealed critical insights into game difficulty and engagement curves.

Key Findings:

  • Early Friction: Levels 1 and 2 showed a steep learning curve, taking 3-4 minutes on average, leading to early drop-offs.
  • The "Mid-Game" Peak: Levels 5-12 showed the highest event counts, indicating sustained engagement.
  • Difficulty Spikes: Level 15 stood out with an average completion time of 3 minutes (vs 1.5 mins for late levels), indicating a potential design bottleneck.
Figure 3: Average time spent per level, highlighting the spike at Level 15.

Results & Conclusion

The study confirmed that implicit learning works. Children who engaged more actively with Fooya!'s mechanics (e.g., using "healthy food" shields) were statistically more likely to choose healthy snacks (apples, yogurt) over unhealthy ones (candy, chips) post-intervention.

Strategic Recommendations:

  • Optimize Onboarding: Reduce the difficulty of Levels 1-2 to prevent early churn.
  • Smooth Difficulty Curves: Refine Level 15 to maintain flow state without frustrating players.
  • Personalization: Use telemetry data to provide adaptive feedback, nudging players who frequently choose unhealthy in-game items.
